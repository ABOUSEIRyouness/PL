ðŸ§  Origins of TTT

I learned that Test-Teach-Test was originally introduced by Henry C. Morrison in the 1930s as part of a behaviorist learning model focused on mastery. The goal was simple but powerful: assess what students already know, teach what they need, then test again to ensure learning has taken place. While rooted in literacy and science instruction, it was also extended to language learning.

ðŸ”„ How It Has Evolved

Over time, educators like Scott Thornbury reinterpreted TTT as a more communicative, flexible approach. Instead of traditional tests, learners are now set meaningful communicative tasks. Based on performance, the teacher provides responsive instruction, then asks learners to perform a follow-up task that reflects what they've learned.

ðŸ”— How It Fits Within TBLT

To me, TTT is incredibly compatible with Task-Based Language Teaching. The initial task is used diagnostically (like a TBLT pre-task), followed by focused instruction (form-focused teaching), and then task repetition or extension. It's practically a synonym for what many would call Task-Teach-Task.

ðŸ’¡ Why I Like This Approach

  • It's reactive: You teach based on what learners actually need.
  • It's engaging: Tasks are realistic and communicative.
  • It helps students notice their own gaps and progress.

ðŸ“š A Sample Lesson

For a class about making appointments, I might start with a roleplay where students try to book a doctor's visit. I'd observe and take notes. Then, I'd teach phrases like "I'd like to schedule an appointment" and how to talk about time. Finally, they'd do a more challenging roleplay with added complications.

ðŸ”š Conclusion

TTT has come a long way from its behaviorist roots. When interpreted through a task-based lens, it becomes a powerful, student-centered tool. For me, it's one of the most practical and flexible frameworks to use in any communicative classroom.
